# Education Product Playbook

Use this reference for education, admissions, tutoring, counseling, student planning, parent-facing, school-facing, and learning products.

## Stakeholders

Separate users, buyers, and trust influencers:

- Student: wants clarity, progress, confidence, and less work.
- Parent: wants safety, credibility, outcomes, and transparency.
- Counselor/tutor/teacher: wants leverage, accuracy, workflow fit, and less admin.
- School/organization: wants compliance, reporting, implementation support, and outcomes.
- Platform/community: may influence discovery, trust, and peer comparison.

## Competitor Types

- Human expert: counselor, tutor, consultant, agency, teacher, mentor.
- Information platform: directory, ranking site, forum, resource library.
- Workflow software: planning tool, LMS, CRM, form/report/export tool.
- AI tutor/advisor: chat, recommendation, grading, writing, planning, or agent workflow.
- Content/community: creator, newsletter, social group, paid course.
- Incumbent behavior: spreadsheet, parent network, school counselor, searching manually.

## Education-Specific Dimensions

Compare:

- Trust proof: credentials, case outcomes, citations, testimonials, institutional backing.
- Personalization depth: student profile, goals, constraints, timeline, risk, preferences.
- Data quality: freshness, source transparency, coverage, localization, eligibility logic.
- Outcome link: does the product plausibly improve decisions, learning, application quality, or completion?
- Parent/student UX: clarity, stress reduction, progress visibility, explainability.
- Workflow fit: calendars, exports, reports, counselor handoff, school systems, collaboration.
- Privacy and safety: minors, student data, consent, retention, sensitive attributes.
- Seasonality: admissions cycles, exams, course selection, deadlines, reporting windows.
- Service model: self-serve, expert-assisted, concierge, school contract, hybrid.
- Price and willingness to pay: parent spend, school budget, tutor/consultant substitution.

## Differentiation Wedges

Look for:

- Transparent recommendations with citations and confidence levels.
- Better student-profile fit than generic advice.
- Reports parents can trust and students can act on.
- Hybrid expert handoff for high-stakes moments.
- Workflow completion: plan, tasks, reminders, exports, revisions, progress tracking.
- Localized data and eligibility rules competitors do not cover well.
- Lower-cost alternative to expert services without pretending to replace all expertise.

## Red Flags

- Claims admissions or learning outcomes without proof.
- Treats parent, student, and counselor needs as identical.
- Uses generic advice that competitors can copy.
- Ignores privacy, minors, sensitive data, or consent.
- No plan for trust when AI advice is uncertain.
- No handling of deadlines, seasonality, or changing policies.

## Education Product Recommendation Pattern

End with:

- Primary buyer and primary user.
- Trust claim the product can credibly make.
- Workflow moment where the product enters.
- Human expert or incumbent behavior it displaces or augments.
- Proof needed before scaling.
- Segment to avoid because stakes, regulation, or trust burden are too high.
