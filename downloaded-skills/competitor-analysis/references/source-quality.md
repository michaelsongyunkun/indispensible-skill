# Source Quality

Use this reference when competitor analysis depends on current public facts, pricing, traction, reviews, funding, traffic, product features, or high-stakes decisions.

## Source Hierarchy

Prefer sources in this order:

1. User-provided materials: internal notes, customer feedback, screenshots, pricing sheets, sales notes.
2. Official competitor sources: website, pricing page, docs, changelog, blog, support center, terms, app listing.
3. Product-observable sources: demos, screenshots, public UI, docs examples, app store metadata.
4. Customer voice: reviews, community posts, case studies, testimonials, social comments.
5. Third-party analysis: media, analyst reports, funding databases, traffic/ranking tools.
6. Inference: reasoned conclusions from signals; label clearly.

Use public or authorized sources only. Do not use leaked data, private credentials, hidden tokens, paywalled material the user has not provided, or prohibited scraping paths.

## Evidence Register

For important claims, keep a compact evidence register:

| Claim | Evidence Label | Source Type | Date Checked | Confidence | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Use the skill's evidence labels:

- Confirmed: official or direct source.
- Observed: visible in product, public UI, app listing, reviews, screenshots, demos.
- Reported: credible third-party or customer report.
- Inferred: reasoned from available signals.
- Unknown: not verified.

## Current-Fact Rules

Always verify current facts when they affect the recommendation:

- Pricing, packaging, plan limits, free tier.
- Product launches, AI model claims, integrations, enterprise/security claims.
- Funding, acquisitions, leadership changes, partnerships.
- App ratings, review themes, traffic, popularity, community momentum.

If browsing is unavailable, say what could not be verified and avoid precise claims.

## Confidence Language

Use:

- High confidence: multiple recent primary sources align.
- Medium confidence: one primary source or multiple secondary sources align.
- Low confidence: old source, thin evidence, or inference-heavy.

Avoid:

- Fake precision: invented market share, ARR, user count, or growth rate.
- Unqualified rankings: "best", "largest", "dominant" without evidence.
- Stale pricing: pricing claims without a checked date.

## Triangulation

For each major competitor, try to triangulate at least:

- Positioning from official site.
- Feature depth from docs/product pages.
- Pricing from pricing page or plan descriptions.
- Customer pain from reviews/forums/social/customer notes.
- Momentum from changelog, hiring, funding/news, app reviews, or content cadence.

When only one source exists, label the claim with lower confidence.
