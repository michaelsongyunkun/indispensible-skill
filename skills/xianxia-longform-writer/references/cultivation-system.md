# Cultivation System Design

Use this reference when designing or auditing realms, breakthroughs, techniques, artifacts, pills, formations, combat, bloodlines, or power progression.

## Realm Ladder

For each realm, specify:

| Field | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Name | What is the realm called in the story's language and culture? |
| Threshold | What must change to enter it: body, qi, soul, Dao insight, fate, bloodline, merit, external ritual? |
| Capability | What becomes possible that was impossible before? |
| Constraint | What still cannot be solved at this realm? |
| Cost | What resource, risk, injury, lifespan, karma, emotion, or relationship is consumed? |
| Social effect | Who now fears, recruits, taxes, hunts, marries, or exploits this cultivator? |
| Bottleneck | Why most cultivators fail here. |

Avoid realm names that are only labels. Each realm should alter behavior, status, perception, combat, travel, or politics.

## Breakthrough Checklist

Before allowing a breakthrough, answer:

- Why now, not earlier?
- What bottleneck was overcome?
- What resource or insight made it possible?
- What went wrong or nearly went wrong?
- What changed in the protagonist's body, senses, social danger, or worldview?
- Who notices, benefits, fears, or pays?
- What new limitation appears immediately?

If the answer is only "the protagonist needed to win," redesign the scene.

## Power Economy

Track scarcity:

- Training resources: spirit stones, veins, pills, medicinal fields, beast cores, manuals, masters, arrays, time dilation spaces.
- Authority resources: disciple rank, tokens, bloodline rights, sect contribution, imperial titles, merchant credit, oaths.
- Metaphysical resources: fate, karma, merit, Dao marks, lifespan, soul integrity, heavenly tribulation tolerance.

Resources should move through the story. When gained, lost, stolen, refined, promised, or exposed, update the continuity ledger.

## Techniques And Costs

For every signature technique, define:

- Activation condition.
- Visible tell or sensory signature.
- Range and duration.
- What counters it.
- Cost or aftereffect.
- Why the protagonist cannot spam it.
- How it expresses personality or Dao.

## Combat Design

Xianxia combat should be tactical and consequential:

| Layer | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Objective | Kill, delay, expose, protect, seize, escape, test, bait, complete ritual. |
| Information | What each side knows, misunderstands, conceals, or discovers mid-fight. |
| Terrain | Formation, weather, ruins, crowd, spirit vein, mortal city, oath-bound stage, sect law. |
| Resource | Qi, talismans, pills, artifacts, injuries, time, reputation, forbidden technique. |
| Turn | The moment the fight's meaning changes. |
| Consequence | Injury, debt, witness, reputation shift, faction response, damaged artifact, new enemy. |

Avoid fights that are only bigger attacks colliding. Give each combatant a plan.

## System Audit

Before finishing a plan or chapter, check:

- No ability appears only when convenient.
- Higher realms still feel dangerous without making lower-realm scenes meaningless.
- Rare resources stay rare.
- Training speed has an explained cost.
- Tribulation, bottleneck, or backlash is not decorative.
- Power gains create new problems instead of ending the story's pressure.
