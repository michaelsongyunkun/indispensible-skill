# Prose Flow

Use this reference when supporting characters feel like tools, emotional description repeats, or scene/paragraph transitions feel abrupt.

## Supporting Cast Agency

Every recurring or scene-important side character needs more than a plot function.

| Check | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Desire | What does this character want in this scene besides helping or blocking the protagonist? |
| Pressure | What social, emotional, physical, factional, or practical pressure shapes their behavior? |
| Risk | What could they lose by speaking, staying silent, helping, lying, or leaving? |
| Friction | Where do they misunderstand, resist, delay, bargain, joke, judge, envy, or protect? |
| Trace | What small habit, value, wound, competence, or contradiction makes them memorable? |

## Tool-Character Audit

If a supporting character feels like a device, revise with one of these moves:

- Give them a private objective that intersects with the plot objective.
- Let them reveal information incompletely, reluctantly, wrongly, or with a price.
- Let them act from loyalty to someone other than the protagonist.
- Let them be competent in one area and blind in another.
- Give them a consequence after they serve the plot.
- Let their voice, timing, silence, or body language show relationship pressure.

If removing the plot clue removes the entire person, rebuild the character.

## Emotion Beat Variation

Repeated emotion usually comes from reusing the same channel. Rotate channels:

| Channel | Use |
| --- | --- |
| Body | Breath, throat, hands, posture, appetite, fatigue, pain, heat, cold. |
| Perception | What the character notices, ignores, misreads, or cannot look at. |
| Speech | Shorter answers, over-formality, jokes, deflection, interruption, wrong address. |
| Action | Cleaning, packing, training harder, avoiding a door, touching an object, changing route. |
| Memory | A triggered image that complicates the current feeling. |
| Social behavior | Deference, distance, provocation, service, refusal, asking a smaller question. |

Do not repeat the same emotional signal unless repetition itself is the point and changes meaning.

## Emotional Progression

A scene should not hold one flat emotion. Track movement:

```text
Trigger -> first reaction -> suppression/misread -> choice -> leak/shift -> aftereffect
```

Examples of progression:

- Fear becomes anger because the character notices who benefits.
- Shame becomes politeness because status makes honesty dangerous.
- Grief becomes practical care because crisis allows no ceremony.
- Relief becomes suspicion because the rescue arrives too easily.

## Transition Bridge

Use one bridge when moving between beats or scenes:

- **Cause bridge**: The next beat happens because of the previous choice or consequence.
- **Time bridge**: Mark elapsed time through fatigue, light, weather, travel, healing, or routine.
- **Place bridge**: Carry an object, sound, smell, wound, or social rule into the new location.
- **Emotion bridge**: Let the last scene's feeling color the first observation of the next.
- **Contrast bridge**: Cut sharply only when the contrast creates meaning.
- **Question bridge**: End one beat with a question the next beat pressures or reframes.

Abrupt cuts are fine when intentional. They feel rough when the reader cannot tell why this moment follows the previous one.

## Revision Moves

- Add one sentence of emotional residue before a location change.
- Replace a repeated emotion cue with a choice or avoidance behavior.
- Give a side character a cost for the information they provide.
- Convert a hard exposition jump into a reaction beat, object handoff, or interrupted task.
- Trim a side character who only repeats known information.
- Let a transition carry a wound, debt, object, question, or changed form of address.
