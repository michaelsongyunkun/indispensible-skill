# Psychological Depth

Use this reference when a chapter lacks psychological description/心理描写, characters make plot-correct choices without enough inner pressure, or emotional turns feel stated rather than lived.

## Inner Conflict Chain

Psychology becomes readable when it forms a chain:

```text
Stimulus -> biased interpretation -> inner conflict -> choice/delay/mistake -> aftereffect
```

Ask what collides inside the character:

- Desire vs fear.
- Pride vs need.
- Loyalty vs survival.
- Shame vs anger.
- Mercy vs revenge.
- Ambition vs moral line.
- Old wound vs current evidence.

Do not write only "he was conflicted." Let the conflict alter attention, speech, timing, and action.

## Perception Bias

A character's mind should filter the scene. Psychology often appears through what the character notices or misreads.

Use:

- Selective attention: the one object, face, wound, scent, tone, or silence they cannot ignore.
- Misreading: they assume contempt, betrayal, pity, threat, affection, or weakness because of old pressure.
- Avoidance: they refuse to look, answer, touch, remember, or ask.
- Overreaction: a small detail triggers disproportionate anger, fear, tenderness, or caution.
- Reframing: after a reveal, the same object or line means something different.

## Decision Pressure

Inner life should push the scene forward. Tie psychology to decisions:

- What does the character want to do but cannot admit?
- What tactically smart move do they refuse for emotional reasons?
- What do they delay because the answer would hurt?
- What promise, debt, fear, or shame changes the plan?
- What private motive makes a public action sharper?

The strongest psychological beat often appears as a choice, not an explanation.

## Subtext Gap

Build tension between layers:

| Layer | Question |
| --- | --- |
| Said | What does the character allow others to hear? |
| Thought | What do they understand but hide? |
| Felt | What emotion arrives before they can control it? |
| Done | What action betrays or contradicts the spoken line? |

Use the gap to avoid repetitive emotional labels. A calm sentence can carry rage if the hand, attention, or next choice betrays it.

## Psychological Aftereffect

Important events should leave residue.

After a threat, victory, loss, rescue, betrayal, breakthrough, or public humiliation, show one aftereffect:

- Body: sleep, appetite, breath, pain, stillness, tremor, cultivation backlash.
- Attention: what the character keeps checking, avoiding, counting, or listening for.
- Speech: shorter lines, wrong title, formal distance, sudden honesty, forced joke.
- Relationship: trust loosens, debt deepens, intimacy becomes awkward, suspicion changes target.
- Choice: they prepare differently, spare someone, strike first, ask for help, or refuse the old compromise.

Aftereffect lets psychology accumulate across chapters instead of resetting after each plot event.

## Revision Moves

When psychology feels thin:

- Add one biased perception before a major decision.
- Replace an emotion label with stimulus -> interpretation -> choice.
- Let a memory be triggered by present action, not inserted as background summary.
- Give silence a reason: fear, strategy, shame, loyalty, desire, or calculation.
- Add one aftereffect in the next scene so the event changes behavior.
- Keep psychological beats close to action; do not stop the chapter for long self-analysis.
