# Scene Balance

Use this reference when prose feels tiring, clue-heavy, symbol-heavy, always high-pressure, or when characters feel like tools for delivering evidence.

## Information Density

Readers can track a dense scene when one element leads and the others support it. Before drafting, choose:

| Budget | Guideline |
| --- | --- |
| New facts | Foreground 1-2 important facts per scene. Move extra lore, factions, clues, or rules to later scenes. |
| Symbols | Let one motif dominate. Other motifs may appear only as texture unless they change the action. |
| Mysteries | Advance one main mystery and optionally brush one secondary mystery. Do not open several major questions at once. |
| Names | Limit new named people, places, artifacts, and institutions in the same passage. |
| Explanations | Give the reader enough to act emotionally now; save taxonomy for when it changes a decision. |

If many objects or symbols matter, sequence them across beats: notice -> misread -> use -> consequence -> later reinterpretation.

## Symbol Budget

Charged motifs work best when they take turns. For scenes with recurring objects such as accounts, shadows, lamps, wells, or signatures:

- Assign one lead symbol to carry the scene's emotional or plot turn.
- Let secondary symbols stay in the background unless a character acts on them.
- Avoid naming several motifs in consecutive sentences unless confusion or ritual overload is the intended effect.
- Reintroduce motifs through changed context: who touches it, who refuses to look, what it now costs, what memory it triggers.

## Pressure Release

Pressure release is not filler. A breather chapter should change at least one quiet thing:

- Relationship: trust, debt, jealousy, tenderness, apology, shared secret, changed address.
- Strategy: new plan, abandoned plan, moral line, private vow, alliance terms.
- Body: injury care, exhaustion, hunger, sleep, training soreness, failed recovery.
- World texture: routine, market, meal, lesson, repair, travel, weather, small sect politics.
- Emotion: grief delayed by crisis, shame, relief, doubt, desire, homesickness, fear of power.

Use a breather chapter after repeated shocks, battles, revelations, or public confrontations. Keep a small hook, but let the main pleasure be settling consequence into character.

## Character Agency Check

Before using a character to reveal a clue, answer:

- What does this character want in the scene?
- What are they afraid will happen if they speak plainly?
- What do they hide, distort, bargain for, or misunderstand?
- What relationship pressure shapes how they deliver information?
- What cost do they pay for revealing, withholding, or misreading the evidence?
- How would the scene change if they refused to cooperate?

If removing the clue removes the character's entire purpose, redesign them. Give the character a personal stake, private rhythm, flaw, affection, debt, vanity, loyalty, or contradiction.

## Revision Moves

When a passage feels overloaded:

- Split one dense scene into discovery, aftermath, and decision.
- Convert one exposition block into a character choice under social pressure.
- Remove or delay the least urgent clue.
- Replace a repeated symbol mention with a bodily reaction or relationship beat.
- Let a witness be wrong, evasive, proud, ashamed, or protecting someone.
- Follow a revelation with a quiet consequence before the next crisis.

## Audit

Before final output, check:

- Can the reader name the scene's lead clue or symbol?
- Does every important fact alter action, emotion, strategy, or relationship?
- Is there a pause after major pressure, or only another pressure spike?
- Does each clue-bearing character remain a person with desire and risk?
- Would the scene still have emotional value if the clue were removed?
