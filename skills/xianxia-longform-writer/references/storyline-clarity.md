# Storyline Clarity

Use this reference when a story feels scattered, episodic, hard to summarize, or when an outline has many events but no clear throughline.

## Narrative Spine

Before expanding a premise, write the main line in one sentence:

```text
[Protagonist] wants [visible goal], but [opposition/pressure] forces [costly action], so they must [escalating strategy] before [stakes deadline or irreversible loss].
```

If this sentence cannot be written, the story is not ready for a long outline. Clarify the goal, pressure, strategy, and stakes first.

## Plotline Map

Use this compact structure for outlines and diagnostics:

| Field | Purpose |
| --- | --- |
| Main line | The protagonist's central pursuit across the arc. |
| Central question | The reader-facing question that stays alive. |
| Starting pressure | The event, debt, threat, desire, or discovery that makes inaction impossible. |
| Goal ladder | Immediate goal -> mini-arc goal -> volume goal -> series goal. |
| Opposition ladder | Rival -> institution/faction -> realm/world rule -> hidden final pressure. |
| Turning points | Moments that irreversibly change status, knowledge, relationship, danger, or power. |
| Payoff line | What emotional, tactical, or thematic promise the climax must fulfill. |

## Causal Beat Chain

For every major beat, fill this sentence:

```text
Because [previous choice/reveal/consequence], [character] does [action], which causes [new pressure/reversal].
```

Prefer causal beats over event lists:

- Weak: "He enters the sect. He meets a rival. He finds a manual. He joins a trial."
- Strong: "Because he exposes fake medicine in the market, the rival clan blocks his sect entry; to prove eligibility he accepts a dangerous trial, where the hidden manual becomes both evidence and bait."

## Subplot Discipline

Each subplot must do at least one job for the main line:

- Intensify the central conflict.
- Reveal a hidden cost or lie.
- Change a relationship needed for the climax.
- Delay the goal in a way that forces a better strategy.
- Provide a tool, debt, wound, or promise that pays off later.
- Mirror or test the protagonist's false belief.

Cut, merge, or postpone subplots that only add flavor without changing decisions.

## Long-Form Arc Grid

For long novels, keep this visible:

| Unit | Main question | Required turn |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Chapter | What immediate problem must be solved now? | A choice, reveal, loss, bargain, injury, public status change, or new constraint. |
| Mini-arc | What short-term goal is being pursued? | The protagonist gains or loses leverage. |
| Volume | What status/world change is promised? | The arena changes: rank, faction, realm, enemy class, truth, or moral position. |
| Series | What ultimate contradiction drives the story? | The protagonist's final power answers or corrupts the original desire. |

## Clarity Audit

Before final output, check:

- The reader can state what the protagonist is trying to do right now.
- The current chapter's goal connects to the mini-arc goal.
- The mini-arc goal connects to the volume goal.
- A setback changes the protagonist's strategy, not just the difficulty level.
- The antagonist or faction pressure has a plan, not only an attitude.
- Subplots return to affect choices.
- Reveals create new action, not only new information.
- The climax pays off the central question rather than only staging a big event.
