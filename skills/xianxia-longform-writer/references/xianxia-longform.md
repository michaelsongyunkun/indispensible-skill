# Long-Form Xianxia Planning

Use this reference for full novels, long serials, volume planning, major arc design, or 修仙/玄幻 projects where world rules and reader expectation must hold for hundreds of chapters.

## First Decisions

Define these before drafting a large arc:

| Decision | What to specify |
| --- | --- |
| Reader promise | The durable pleasure: ascent, revenge, sect intrigue, survival, mystery, romance, clan-building, empire-building, anti-hero freedom, cosmic awe. |
| Protagonist engine | Surface goal, deeper hunger, wound/false belief, forbidden desire, moral line, and what power threatens to turn them into. |
| World pressure | Why the protagonist cannot train safely forever: enemies, deadlines, resource scarcity, sect exams, calamities, vows, debts, hidden bloodline, fate. |
| Series question | The question that survives many volumes, such as "Who stole the heavenly mandate?" or "Can mortal cultivation resist the immortal court?" |
| Tone promise | Choose the balance: gritty survival, hot-blooded ascent, elegant Dao mystery, ruthless demonic path, political sect drama, romantic cultivation. |

## Scale Architecture

Plan at four scales at once:

| Scale | Job |
| --- | --- |
| Chapter | One immediate objective, conflict, turn, and hook. |
| Mini-arc | 5-15 chapters around a trial, hunt, duel, marketplace deal, secret realm, investigation, escape, or tournament. |
| Volume | 40-100 chapters that transform status: outer disciple to inner disciple, local city to sect, sect to region, lower realm to upper realm. |
| Series | The long promise: truth of the world, Dao conflict, fate, final enemy, ascension, or moral transformation. |

Each scale should have an active question. Example: "Can he survive the alchemy hall tonight?" "Who sabotaged the furnace?" "Can he win inner disciple status?" "Why does his fire technique answer to a dead god?"

## Common Xianxia Engines

Use one or combine two:

- **Ascent engine**: The protagonist climbs social and metaphysical ranks, but each realm reveals a harsher hierarchy.
- **Debt engine**: Favors, vows, revenge, karma, oaths, and teacher-disciple obligations force choices.
- **Mystery engine**: Techniques, bloodlines, reincarnation, ruins, and heavenly law reveal deeper contradictions.
- **Faction engine**: Sects, clans, merchants, dynasties, demon paths, and immortal courts compete for resources and legitimacy.
- **Dao engine**: Power requires an answer to a personal contradiction, not just more energy.
- **Survival engine**: The protagonist is hunted, exiled, poisoned, bound by curse, or trapped in a hostile realm.

## Volume Template

For each volume, define:

- Starting status: rank, relationships, enemies, resources, injuries, public reputation.
- Promise: what the reader expects to see resolved or transformed.
- New arena: sect, city, ruin, battlefield, upper realm, academy, prison, auction, mortal kingdom.
- Main external conflict: antagonist/faction with a concrete objective.
- Main inner conflict: what power or status tempts the protagonist to become worse.
- Realm movement: breakthrough, failed breakthrough, bottleneck, corrupted path, or cost of rapid progress.
- Midpoint reversal: what changes the rules of the arena.
- Climax: public duel, secret betrayal, breakthrough under pressure, loss, sacrifice, exposure, or irreversible choice.
- Aftermath: who owes whom, who knows what, what resource is spent, what injury remains, what larger power notices.

## Avoid

- Repeating the same tournament/auction/secret-realm pattern without a new relationship or world consequence.
- Treating arrogant young masters as disposable noise; give major rivals motives, patrons, constraints, and memory.
- Letting the protagonist gain treasures without cost, witnesses, enemies, debt, or altered strategy.
- Making every elder foolish so the protagonist can win cheaply.
- Resolving world mysteries too early; reveal partial truths that create sharper questions.
